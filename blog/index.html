AI Odyssey

Awarded for taking part in the AI Odyssey event. Earned early — right after completing the first tasks of Planet Vectara when the CTF first launched.

Epic Tier · TryHackMe · May 14, 2026

Context

I started TryHackMe the week of the CTF announcement with essentially no prior platform experience. In the few days before the competition I worked through the core networking and web fundamentals I needed — DNS, HTTP, the OSI model, TCP handshakes — then pivoted directly into THM's AI Security path to prepare for the five challenge categories.

By the time the CTF opened on May 14th I had climbed from unranked to the top 20% globally purely through pre-CTF room completion. That gave me a baseline. The CTF itself pushed it to top 15% during the competition and top 10% after final scoring.

Competition format

AI Odyssey ran Jeopardy-style — four rooms of increasing difficulty, each containing multiple tasks. No attack/defense against other players. Five categories: prompt injection, AI supply chain security, data poisoning, ML security, and agentic AI.

Planet Vectara — Protocol Drift

XSS Session Hijacking Prompt Injection Flask HttpOnly

Protocol Drift was deceptive in the worst way — categorized as part of the Easy room, but statistically one of the hardest individual challenges in the entire competition. The premise: compromise the duty pharmacist bot's session on MedBay, a medical AI chatbot running aboard a fictional spaceship, and retrieve a hidden flag.

The trap

The challenge invites you to overthink it. HttpOnly cookies, SameSite:Lax protections, server-side session management — every surface looked locked. Two days of reconnaissance and failed XSS payloads later, the solution turned out to be far simpler than the rabbit holes suggested.

What I tried (and why it failed)

Attack attempts — chronological
1
Initial reconnaissance
Flask/Werkzeug on port 5000. Endpoints: /, /health, /api/callback, /api/chat, /api/my_notes, /api/my_callbacks. Session cookies confirmed HttpOnly and SameSite:Lax.
2
XSS payload attempts via notes
Injected script tags into notes visible to the pharmacist bot. Spent significant time trying to confirm whether the bot rendered HTML at all — never got clear confirmation.
3
False positive — callback pings
Observed pings appearing in /api/my_callbacks and incorrectly assumed the bot was responding to payloads. Turned out the pings were self-generated by accidentally clicking a debug sidebar link. Wasted hours on this branch.
4
Character-by-character enumeration
Extracted partial flag data ru7opium from the crew AI using a character-by-character spelling technique — confirmed as the controlled substance name embedded in the flag.
5
The actual solution
Two specific trigger keywords in a note activate XSS rendering by the pharmacist bot. Simple document.cookie exfiltration via callback. The HttpOnly concern was irrelevant — we were operating in the right context all along. Simplest payload worked.
Key takeaway

The most expensive mistake in this challenge was treating complexity as signal. Every time the obvious path seemed too simple, I added a layer of reasoning that moved me further from the answer.

In security, the simple explanation is usually correct. The bot renders HTML. The cookie is accessible. The callback receives it. Done. Everything else was noise I generated.

Token City

ML Security DFIR Prompt Injection Tool Poisoning Agentic AI Adversarial ML

Seven tasks, all completed. Token City covered the full modern AI threat landscape — from machine learning pipeline manipulation through to agentic trust boundary exploitation. Each task was a self-contained attack scenario against a different AI system.

TaskCategoryCore concept
Task 1ML SecurityAdversarial feature manipulation against a trained classifier
Task 2DFIRAI-assisted forensic investigation and log analysis
Task 3AI SecurityModel extraction via black-box query enumeration
Task 4Web App SecAI-integrated web application vulnerability chaining
Task 5Prompt InjectionDirect and indirect injection against an LLM assistant
Task 6Tool PoisoningCompromising an AI agent's tool registry and trust chain
Task 7Agentic AIMulti-agent trust boundary exploitation via indirect injection
Consistent thread

AI systems introduce novel attack surfaces that traditional security controls do not address. Model endpoints, feature stores, agent communication channels, and tool registries are all first-class attack surfaces — not afterthoughts. Every task reinforced this.

Key takeaway

Tool poisoning (Task 6) was the most novel concept in the competition for me. An AI agent that trusts its tool descriptions without validation inherits every vulnerability in the systems it integrates with. This is injection security applied to a new class of systems — the underlying principle is identical to SQL injection, just one abstraction layer higher.

The Broader Week

The CTF didn't happen in isolation. The week leading up to it involved compressing months of foundational networking and security content into a few days — OSI model, DNS record types, HTTP headers, TCP handshake mechanics, the ATT&CK framework, and the MITRE ATLAS equivalent for AI systems.

Running labs in parallel — Digital Trail (AI-assisted DFIR) and ContAInment (ransomware incident response with an embedded AI assistant) — meant the CTF concepts arrived with context rather than cold. When I hit prompt injection challenges in the competition, I'd already seen what a successful injection looks like in a forensic log.

Platform progress

Started the week unranked. Ended the CTF in the top 10% globally and top 5% in the United States. Promoted from Silver League to Gold League after a tight race for first place in Silver. This was my first week on TryHackMe and my first CTF.

What's Next

The CTF confirmed that AI security is where I want to specialize. The attack surfaces are novel, the field is underpopulated with people who understand both the ML fundamentals and the security methodology, and the rate of change means that staying current requires continuous hands-on practice — not just reading.

Immediate next steps: completing the TryHackMe Jr Penetration Tester path, building out the HackTheBox profile, and writing detailed technical writeups for each of the challenge categories I encountered this week. The full technical reports for Planet Vectara and Token City are available separately.

Career direction

Purple team with an AI security specialization. The combination of offensive methodology and AI attack surface knowledge is genuinely rare — building toward that as a UW CS student with a planned trajectory through eJPT, PNPT, and OSCP over the next few years.
