Context

I joined TryHackMe the week the CTF was announced with no prior platform experience. In the days before the competition I worked through the networking and web fundamentals I needed — DNS, HTTP, the OSI model, TCP handshakes — then moved into THM's AI Security path to cover the five challenge categories.

By the time the CTF opened on May 14th I'd gone from unranked to the top 20% globally just through pre-CTF room completions. The CTF itself pushed it to top 15% mid-competition and top 10% after final scoring.

Competition format

AI Odyssey ran Jeopardy-style — four rooms of increasing difficulty, each with multiple tasks. No attack/defense against other players. Five categories: prompt injection, AI supply chain security, data poisoning, ML security, and agentic AI.

Planet Vectara — Protocol Drift

XSS Session Hijacking Prompt Injection Flask HttpOnly

Protocol Drift was deceptive in the worst way — labeled part of the Easy room, but statistically one of the hardest individual challenges in the whole competition. The premise: compromise the duty pharmacist bot's session on MedBay, a medical AI chatbot running aboard a fictional spaceship, and retrieve a hidden flag.

The trap

The challenge makes you want to overthink it. HttpOnly cookies, SameSite:Lax protections, server-side session management — every surface looked locked. Two days of recon and failed XSS payloads later, the solution was much simpler than any of that suggested.

What I tried (and why it failed)

Attack attempts — chronological
1
Initial reconnaissance
Flask/Werkzeug on port 5000. Endpoints: /, /health, /api/callback, /api/chat, /api/my_notes, /api/my_callbacks. Session cookies confirmed HttpOnly and SameSite:Lax.
2
XSS payload attempts via notes
Injected script tags into notes visible to the pharmacist bot. Spent a lot of time trying to confirm whether the bot rendered HTML at all — never got a clear answer.
3
False positive — callback pings
Saw pings appearing in /api/my_callbacks and assumed the bot was responding to payloads. They were self-generated — I'd accidentally clicked a debug sidebar link. Wasted hours on that branch.
4
Character-by-character enumeration
Extracted partial flag data ru7opium from the crew AI using a character-by-character spelling technique — confirmed as the controlled substance name embedded in the flag.
5
The actual solution
Two specific trigger keywords in a note activate XSS rendering by the pharmacist bot. Simple document.cookie exfiltration via callback. The HttpOnly concern was irrelevant — we were in the right context the whole time. The simplest payload worked.
Key takeaway

The most expensive mistake here was treating complexity as a signal. Every time the obvious path seemed too easy, I added reasoning that pushed me further from the answer.

In security, the simple explanation is usually right. The bot renders HTML. The cookie is accessible. The callback receives it. Done. Everything else was noise I created.

Token City

ML Security DFIR Prompt Injection Tool Poisoning Agentic AI Adversarial ML

Seven tasks, all completed. Token City covered the modern AI threat landscape — from machine learning pipeline manipulation through to agentic trust boundary exploitation. Each task was a self-contained attack scenario against a different AI system.

Task 6Tool PoisoningCompromising an AI agent's tool registry and trust chain
Task 7Agentic AIMulti-agent trust boundary exploitation via indirect injection
Consistent thread

AI systems have attack surfaces that traditional security controls don't cover. Model endpoints, feature stores, agent communication channels, and tool registries are all real attack surfaces — not afterthoughts. Every task drove that point home.

Key takeaway

Tool poisoning (Task 6) was the most new concept in the competition for me. An AI agent that trusts its tool descriptions without validation inherits every vulnerability in the systems it integrates with. It's injection security applied to a new class of systems — the underlying principle is identical to SQL injection, just one abstraction layer higher.

The Broader Week

The CTF was part of a bigger week. The days leading up to it involved compressing a lot of foundational networking and security content into a short window — OSI model, DNS record types, HTTP headers, TCP handshake mechanics, the ATT&CK framework, and the MITRE ATLAS equivalent for AI systems.

Running labs in parallel — Digital Trail (AI-assisted DFIR) and ContAInment (ransomware incident response with an embedded AI assistant) — meant I wasn't going into CTF challenges cold. When I hit prompt injection tasks in the competition, I'd already seen what a successful injection looks like in a forensic log.

Platform progress

Started the week unranked. Ended the CTF in the top 10% globally and top 5% in the United States. Promoted from Silver League to Gold League after a tight race for first place in Silver. This was my first week on TryHackMe and my first CTF.

What's Next

The CTF confirmed that AI security is where I want to specialize. The attack surfaces are new, the field is underpopulated with people who understand both ML fundamentals and security methodology, and the pace of change means staying current requires hands-on practice — not just reading.

Next up: finishing the TryHackMe Jr Penetration Tester path, building out a HackTheBox profile, and writing proper technical writeups for each challenge category I hit this week. The full technical reports for Planet Vectara and Token City are available separately.

Career direction

Offensive security with an AI security focus. The combination of offensive methodology and AI attack surface knowledge is rare — working toward that as a UW CS student, with eJPT, PNPT, and OSCP on the roadmap over the next few years.
